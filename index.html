What drives the forecast — variance decomposition

Share of each variable's forecast-error variance attributable to each shock group, by horizon. Hover for shares.
How to read this. Each bar splits the uncertainty in a variable's forecast into the share coming from each family of shocks. A tall navy segment for GDP, for example, means most of the uncertainty about future GDP comes from real-activity shocks rather than from prices or financial conditions. Near term, a variable's own shocks dominate; further out, other blocks contribute more as the dynamics propagate. The split uses a recursive (Cholesky) ordering, so the exact shares depend on that ordering — but the broad ranking (which shocks matter most) is generally robust.

How the model works — structural impulse responses

Response of each variable (rows) to the shock shown at the top of each column, over 20 quarters. Shaded area is the 68% band; for log-level variables responses are % deviations, for rates they are percentage points. The red cells on the diagonal are the shocked variable itself, labelled with the shock size. Hover for values.
Shock sizes (natural units): Real GDP +1% · PCE prices +1% · Unemployment +1 pp · Fed funds +50 bp · 10-year yield +50 bp. These are fixed, policy-scale shocks chosen to be easy to read — they are not normalised to one standard deviation, so they are not equal-sized as historical surprises (a 1% jump in GDP is a far larger surprise than 50bp on the funds rate). Because the model is linear, the response bands scale in proportion to the shock, so their relative width is the same whatever size you pick.

How to read this. Each small chart traces how one variable responds, quarter by quarter, to that shock to the variable named at the top of its column. The shocks are structurally identified by conditional forecasting (CEGSQ): the shocked variable is held on a shocked path while the slow-moving real variables are kept at their no-shock path for the first quarter (a recursive contemporaneous restriction); the response is the difference from the no-shock forecast. Caveat. On the 1986-onward sample the federal-funds column is demand-attenuated — the historical comovement of interest rates and activity is strong and is not fully removed by a one-quarter restriction, so a funds-rate shock does not trace a clean monetary tightening. That reflects the variable mix and sample, not a flaw in the identification machinery, and is a known item for model refinement.

Methodology

This page publishes a regularly updated forecast for the United States from a 24-variable Bayesian vector autoregression (BVAR), estimated on quarterly Federal Reserve Economic Data (FRED) back to 1986 with four lags.

The model uses order-invariant stochastic volatility (Chan-Koop-Yu 2024) on top of a fixed Minnesota coefficient prior and a sum-of-coefficients prior. The stochastic-volatility specification, which is outlier-robust, lets the width of the uncertainty bands respond to the macroeconomic environment rather than being fixed. The posterior is sampled by Gibbs (10,000 draws, 5,000 burn-in, thinned to 1,000).

The headline path is conditioned on a central analyst baseline for the near-term quarters: those values are imposed through a Kalman filter and a full companion-state Carter-Kohn smoother (the dashed navy line), while the model's own unconditional view is shown for context (grey). The long run is anchored to the baseline's annual averages by entropic tilting rather than hard pins, so the bands stay smooth. Shaded areas are 66% credible bands; following central-bank fan-chart convention they fan out from the last observed data point.

The variance decomposition attributes each variable's forecast uncertainty to groups of structural shocks under a recursive ordering. This is a statistical model output, not advice; long-horizon interest-rate paths are the least reliable element and should be read as indicative.
