What drives the forecast — variance decomposition

Share of each variable's forecast-error variance attributable to each shock group, by horizon. Hover for shares.
How to read this. Each bar splits the uncertainty in a variable's forecast into the share coming from each family of shocks. A tall navy segment for GDP, for example, means most of the uncertainty about future GDP comes from real-activity shocks rather than from prices or financial conditions. Near term, a variable's own shocks dominate; further out, other blocks contribute more as the dynamics propagate. The split uses a recursive (Cholesky) ordering, so the exact shares depend on that ordering — but the broad ranking (which shocks matter most) is generally robust.

How the economy responds to shocks — impulse responses

The same model under two different ways of pinning down a structural shock: a recursive (timing) ordering and sign restrictions. That the two methods agree where both are valid is a check on the structural machinery; each is shown below with its own commentary and caveats.

Recursive identification (CEGSQ) — financial shocks

Each financial shock (a row) traced across the five headline responses (columns), over 12 quarters. Each column has its own y-axis — % deviation for output/prices, percentage points for rates. Hover for values.
Shock sizes (illustrative episode magnitudes): Risk-off (credit spread +0.5pp) · Uncertainty (policy uncertainty +50%) · Equity sell-off (equities -10%). Because the model is linear, the responses scale in proportion to the shock.

Which shocks, and why these. These are the shocks this model identifies reliably — financial / risk shocks. Identification is by conditional forecasting (CEGSQ): the shocked financial variable is moved while the slow-moving real variables are held at their no-shock path for the first quarter (a recursive contemporaneous restriction), and the response is the difference from the no-shock forecast. Because financial variables are “fast” (they can react within the quarter), this ordering identifies them cleanly. Not shown: a monetary-policy shock (on the post-1986 sample the rate–activity comovement is demand-dominated, so a recursive funds-rate shock comes out wrong-signed) and an oil-supply shock (it requires sign restrictions plus an oil-production series this model does not carry). See the methodology note. One caveat shared by all the responses: the model is in levels, so a shock’s effect on a level (output, prices) is persistent rather than fading to zero.

Sign-restricted identification — demand & monetary

An aggregate demand shock and a monetary shock (rows), each traced across the same five responses (columns), identified by sign restrictions — a different method than the recursive panel above — over 12 quarters. Demand is normalised to GDP +1% at impact, monetary to a +50bp funds-rate rise. Shaded area is the 68% band. Hover for values.
Why this is here. The panel above identifies shocks recursively (a timing assumption). Sign restrictions are a different scheme: keep every model whose responses match an assumed sign pattern (a demand shock raises output and prices, etc.). That the two methods agree on the demand shock — output, prices and employment all move the textbook way — is a good check that the structural machinery works. The monetary shock is the interesting case: the recursive panel can’t identify it on this sample (the rate–activity comovement is demand-dominated, so a recursive funds-rate shock comes out wrong-signed), whereas sign restrictions — by imposing the uncontroversial contractionary pattern (a rate rise that cools output and prices) — recover the textbook transmission. The honest cost: it is set-identified, so the bands are wider and the pattern is partly assumed rather than discovered (only a minority of admissible models satisfy it — acceptance Demand 98%, Monetary 36%).

The long-run view

Where each forecast object takes the economy over the next decade, and whether the long-run relationships hold together. Hover or tap a chip to flip that forecast's nested 68/90% bands. Coherence checks, not accuracy claims.
This module tracks where each published forecast takes the economy over the next decade, and whether the long-run relationships that should hold the system together actually do. Four forecasts appear, named by where their discipline comes from. Model baseline is the model's own forecast under the standard statistical prior, with no outside information — the benchmark for what anchoring adds. Theory-anchored — new — builds long-run discipline into the model itself, through a prior that holds economically meaningful relationships (spreads, expenditure shares) together rather than adjusting the output afterwards. Benchmark-anchored reweights the model's paths toward public long-run benchmarks (CBO potential growth, FRED long-run rates). Analyst-anchored is the headline forecast, conditioned on the analyst baseline near term and anchored to its long-run views. The theory-anchored forecast is a cross-check whose role is fixed in advance. Long-run accuracy cannot be scored in real time at these horizons, so the diagnostics here are coherence checks against theory and public benchmarks, not accuracy claims.

Coherence checks
How to read this panel. Each chart shows an economically meaningful combination of variables — a spread or a great ratio — with its history (black) and each prior's forecast of it. Theory says the spreads and the output gap are stationary: a coherent long-run forecast keeps them fluctuating around a stable level, and a line that walks away is the model quietly pulling the system apart (this is exactly where an unanchored levels VAR fails first). The labour and consumption shares are different: in the post-1986 data they trend, so a forecast that keeps trending is the model being right, not drifting — the subtitle on each panel says which reading applies. The comparison shown isolates the new prior: the production prior (SOC) versus the long-run prior (PLR) on otherwise identical models. Watch the output-gap panel: the model baseline lets the gap wander several points over the decade; the theory-anchored forecast holds it near zero. That discipline — not any hard-coded number — is what the new object contributes.

Methodology

This page publishes a regularly updated forecast for the United States from a 28-variable Bayesian vector autoregression (BVAR), estimated on quarterly Federal Reserve Economic Data (FRED) back to 1986 with four lags.

The model uses order-invariant stochastic volatility (Chan-Koop-Yu 2024) on top of a fixed Minnesota coefficient prior and a sum-of-coefficients prior. The stochastic-volatility specification, which is outlier-robust, lets the width of the uncertainty bands respond to the macroeconomic environment rather than being fixed. The posterior is sampled by Gibbs (10,000 draws, 5,000 burn-in, thinned to 1,000).

The page shows four forecasts that differ only in what is assumed, named by where each one's discipline comes from:

  • Benchmark-anchored (navy, headline). The model's own dynamics, with the long run gently steered toward published reference values — the Congressional Budget Office's potential growth and natural rate of unemployment, the FOMC's longer-run policy rate, and market-based inflation expectations — by entropic tilting at the five-year horizon. Nothing is imposed in the near term; the anchoring is a soft reweighting of the model's own paths, not a hard pin, so the bands stay smooth.
  • Model baseline (grey). The same model with nothing imposed. It fits the data and short-run dynamics well, but a levels VAR has no structure forcing convergence, so at long horizons it drifts (rates and inflation tend to climb). Shown honestly as the un-anchored baseline; its long horizon is the least reliable element.
  • Theory-anchored (green). The same model with long-run economic relationships — spreads, expenditure shares, the output gap — built into the prior itself (a "priors for the long run" specification), so the long run is disciplined from inside the model rather than adjusted afterwards. No numeric target is imposed anywhere.
  • Analyst-anchored (red). Conditioned on a central analyst forecast for the near term — those values are imposed as hard quarterly pins through a Kalman filter and a Carter-Kohn smoother for roughly the first two years — and tilted to the analyst's annual averages in the long run.

Shaded areas are 66% credible bands; following central-bank fan-chart convention they fan out from the last observed data point.

The variance decomposition attributes each variable's forecast uncertainty to groups of structural shocks under a recursive ordering. This is a statistical model output, not advice; long-horizon interest-rate paths are the least reliable element and should be read as indicative.
